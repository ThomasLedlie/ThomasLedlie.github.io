Home Lab Networking - Cloudflare ZeroTrust and TrueNAS Scale

Due to the nature of my degree and my interest in Linux and home computers, I purchased a HP Prodesk 600 G2 with an i5-6500T installed. I upgraded the RAM of this PC to have 32GB of LPDDR4, and added an NVME SSD as a boot drive and a 2.5" HDD to serve as bulk storage (this is likely to be swapped for a larger capacity SSD in the near future).

During my time at University I had taught myself how to set up a Samba shared drive on my Desktop PC which could be accessed on my home network, however by using this Mini-PC, I would be able to access my shared drive at any time whilst only consuming minimal load.

I installed TrueNAS Scale onto the HP Mini-PC, which is an open source Linux based NAS operating system, which uses a Web UI to install docker apps. By setting my home router to provide this with a static IP addres, meant that I could easily access my files from any device on my home network.

Driven by wanting to remotely access my home server, and have it mounted as a drive wherever I was in the world, I purchased a domain through Cloudflare and setup the Cloudflare Deamon in TrueNAS Scale. This alongside setting up WARP on my machines which need to access my services remotely, sets up a ZeroTrust network between my machines routed through Cloudflares servers, hence avoiding portforwarding by using a Tunnel between my local network and Cloudflare's servers.

For more information on how this service works, Cloudflare provides excellent documentation here.

EE20194 - Mouse Race Group Coursework

In Year 2 Semester 2 the Module: "" set a group coursework to design, build and compete miniature mose robots against each other in completing a lap of a custom built track in the lab. This mouse was to use search coils to detect the current flow in a wire which was embedded into the track, and it was strongly advised that we use PID control and either an analogue or digital design for the mouse.

This group coursework was worth _% of the grade for this module.

My group opted for a digital design, making use of a Raspberry Pi Pico and its ADC pins to compare volatage levels in the probes. By using op-amps to create both amplifiers and peak detectors, we were able to convert the mA AC volatge indued int each search coil into a detectable DC voltage which the Pi Pico could then comapre and use as the input for its PID control system.

To assist in writing our report, and to ensure that the wiring was correct, after modelling each part of the mouse on a breadboard, I constructed these parts in a KiCad schematic and was then able to model how to seperate each section of the circuits between boards. This made assembly on veroboard of our circuits drastically more efficient and reduced overall errors. After completing the coursework and as a learning exercise I modelled a PCB design which could have been fabricated (see below).

There were unfortunately several issues with our initial design due to having used an atypical peak detector circuit, this resulted in somewhat tempermental op-amps which would require manual calibration of their gain ratio to ensure that the detected signal is correct and not at the voltage rails. Due to this, and a member of my group being AWOL for the duration of the project my group unfortunately did not have sufficient time to fully calibrate our robot, however in spite of that, we were able to complete a lap of the course on the competition date and placed in the top half.

Now refelcting on this coursework, in future I will put more effort into optioneering and researching solutions before construction, which should alleviate the constraints we faced.
